# Research, Interviews & Gathering Technical Content for Product Managers and Software Engineers

## Introduction

Effective technical communication is not just the responsibility of writers. For product managers (PMs) and software engineers, gathering, organizing, and communicating technical content is crucial for stakeholder alignment, onboarding, and user understanding. Module 4 reframes core techniques—research, interviews, and technical content gathering—for professionals deeply embedded in the product lifecycle. PMs and engineers often find themselves authoring specs, user stories, internal documentation, and product guides. Mastering these foundational tasks enhances collaboration, speeds up development cycles, and improves customer satisfaction.

**Real-life Example**: A PM at Atlassian working on Jira must coordinate with engineering, design, and QA teams to document features accurately. They conduct interviews, aggregate engineering notes, and synthesize this into customer-facing release documentation.

## Part 1: The Art and Science of Research

For PMs and engineers, research fuels decisions—from market needs to system architecture. Unlike general writing, technical research here supports build decisions, feature prioritization, and development planning.

### Key Research Techniques:

1. **Competitive Analysis**: Study how other platforms solve similar problems.
2. **Customer Feedback Mining**: Use Intercom, NPS responses, and support tickets.
3. **Technical Landscape Mapping**: Understand API limitations, infrastructure, or compliance boundaries.
4. **Cross-functional Docs**: Pull from design, support, and legal repositories.

**Real-life Example**: Before launching an AI feature, a product team at Notion investigated open-source models, internal infrastructure limits, and existing plugin ecosystems.

## Part 2: Planning and Preparing for Interviews

Interviewing isn’t just for user research. PMs and engineers frequently conduct internal discovery sessions to shape PRDs, align roadmaps, and gather technical feasibility data.

### Steps in Interview Preparation:

1. **Clarify Goals**: Define what you need to learn—dependencies, timelines, blockers.
2. **Know the Domain**: Read prior tickets, repos, and notes.
3. **Design Smart Questions**: Use "What if" or "Why" questions for insights.
4. **Documentation Setup**: Create shared notes or recordings for async collaboration.

**Real-life Example**: A PM at Shopify preparing for Q2 roadmap interviews drafts questions about headless commerce strategies and reviews prior architectural decisions.

## Part 3: Conducting Interviews with Stakeholders and SMEs

Whether syncing with backend leads or customer support, your goal is to extract accurate, actionable insights. Efficient interviewing saves time, reduces miscommunication, and improves delivery.

### Best Practices:

1. **Build Rapport**: Start casual; show you’ve done your homework.
2. **Translate Needs**: Frame technical requests in terms of business outcomes.
3. **Capture Action Items**: Note decisions, blockers, and next steps.
4. **Confirm Assumptions**: Paraphrase inputs and verify.

**Real-life Example**: A software engineer at Stripe interviews a security engineer about PCI scope before writing deployment scripts for a payment feature.

## Part 4: Gathering and Validating Technical Content

From syncing GitHub issues to integrating Swagger docs into Confluence, the content you collect must be validated, structured, and version-controlled.

### Content Gathering Strategies:

1. **Break Down Systems**: Use architecture diagrams or service maps.
2. **Trace Dependencies**: Know what data flows, where, and how often.
3. **Link Repositories and Docs**: Create a single source of truth.
4. **Automate Discovery**: Use tools like Backstage or Postman for live endpoints.

**Real-life Example**: At Netflix, engineers update internal documentation on edge services by linking Swagger specs directly into markdown-based repos.

## Part 5: Collaborating with Cross-functional Teams

Collaboration is where PMs and engineers thrive. Clear, consistent documentation aligns teams and avoids ambiguity.

### Collaboration Tips:

1. **Attend Team Rituals**: Grooming sessions, retros, sprint planning.
2. **Share Drafts Early**: Treat documentation as living, evolving artifacts.
3. **Use Review Workflows**: Assign doc reviewers in GitHub or Google Docs.
4. **Encourage Writing Culture**: Celebrate teams who keep documents fresh.

**Real-life Example**: At Amazon, engineers maintain RFC-style documents in a shared repo where PMs, tech leads, and architects comment inline before sign-off.

## Part 6: Tools and Technologies for Gathering Technical Content

Modern product and engineering workflows rely heavily on integrated toolchains. Knowing what tool to use—and when—can boost collaboration and reduce documentation debt.

### Common Tools:

* **For Research**: Productboard, Mixpanel, Google Trends
* **For Docs**: Confluence, Notion, HackMD
* **For Source Control**: GitHub, GitLab, Bitbucket
* **For Meetings**: Otter.ai, Fathom, Loom

**Real-life Example**: A team at Figma uses Notion for requirements, GitHub for implementation notes, and Loom videos for async design reviews, all centralized in a shared knowledge hub.
